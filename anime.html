What they don't teach you in Japanese class

Pitch accent

Introduction to Japanese pitch

Each mora (kana) in Japanese can be of one of two different pitches - high or low. Each Japanese word has an associated "pitch accent". If a word has n morae, then the accent is an integer k between 0 and n inclusive. Then the pitches of the morae of this word are, with mora numbers marked in order, low(1)-high(2)-high(3)-...-high(k)-low(k+1)-...-low(n) if k>1, high(1)-low(2)-low(3)-...-low(n) if k=1, and low(1)-high(2)-high(3)-...-high(n) if k=0. We place the accent mark (’) after the accented mora like this: たべ’る. Obviously, words with 0 accent will have no accent mark: わたし. The accent never falls on ん, っ (small つ), or the second mora of a long vowel. If an accent is ever in one of these afformentioned illegal positions, it moves backwards until it reaches a legal position. Keep in mind that the same word may have different accents based on their part of speech or grammatical function in a sentence.

Phonology of pitch

In normal speech, the high pitch is approximately a major third higher than the low pitch. As a general rule, a wider gap between high and low pitch indicates greater speaker engagement. In two-morae syllables, that is, long vowels or vowels followed by ん, a low-high pattern is articulated as an unbroken long rising tone, and a high-low pattern is articulated as an unbroken long falling tone. In words with accent not equal to 1, the first low mora can be arbitrarily rendered as high.

Pitch in sentence fragments

A "fragment" describes one unit of continuous, uninterrupted speech. This is typically one sentence or phrase. Each fragment has only one accent. After the first accent in a fragment, all subsequent morae in that fragment have low pitch, meaning all subsequent accents in that fragment are ignored. If the fragment is a question, then the last mora will have a rising (low-high) tone. If the second-to-last mora is accented, the last mora could also be rendered with a rising tone.

Verbs

There are two regular categories of verbs based on pitch. Strong verbs and weak verbs. Strong verbs with n syllables have accent n. Weak verbs have accent 0. These accents may change based on conjugation. Here are some examples:
